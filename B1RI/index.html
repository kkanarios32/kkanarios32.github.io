Definition. Inner Product [B1RI]

  • July 14, 2025
  • Kellen Kanarios

Given a vector space \(V\) and a field \(F\). An inner product is a map \[\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to F\] that satisfies the following three properties:

  • Conjugate symmetry: \(\langle \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {y} \rangle = \overline {\langle \mathbf {y}, \mathbf {x}\rangle }\)
  • Linearity in the first argument: \(\langle a\mathbf {x} + b\mathbf {y}, \mathbf {z} \rangle = a\langle \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {z} \rangle + b \langle \mathbf {y}, \mathbf {z} \rangle \)
  • Positive-definiteness: If \(\mathbf {x}\) is not zero, then \(\langle \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {x} \rangle > 0\)

    Definition. Matrix inner product [WWMR]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    The inner product on \(\mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\), obtained by treating a matrix as a long vector and summing the entrywise products: \[\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {Z}\rangle \ :=\ \sum _{i=1}^{m}\sum _{j=1}^{n}X_{ij}Z_{ij},\] the direct generalization of the vector case \(\langle \mathbf {x},\mathbf {z}\rangle =\sum _i x_i z_i\). It is the inner product whose induced norm is the Frobenius norm, \(\|\mathbf {X}\|_F=\sqrt {\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {X}\rangle }\).

    It has a compact trace form (see Matrix trace): \[\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {Z}\rangle \ =\ \mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}^{\top }\mathbf {Z})\ =\ \mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}\mathbf {Z}^{\top }).\]

    Rotary Position Embeddings (ROPE) [TOD6]

    • July 14, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios
    Notation. For this section, our notion of an embedding is a function \(f\) that takes a token and a position i.e. \(f(x, i)\) is the embedding of token \(x\), occurring at position \(i\).
    💡 Insight. The inner product of two embeddings should only need relative positioning i.e. for any two \(\mathbf {x}\), \(\mathbf {y}\) with positions \(i\) and \(j\) respectively, there should exist some \(g\), such that \[\langle f(\mathbf {x}, i), f(\mathbf {y}, j) \rangle = g(\mathbf {x}, \mathbf {y}, i - j)\] Namely, this can be written as a function of just the relative positioning between the embeddings.

    The simplest transformation that only preserves this relative information are rotations.

    However, rotations are only easily defined for \(\mathbb {R}^2\). To get around this, they simply partition their input 2d slices and apply the rotation via \[\mathrm {ROT} = \begin {bmatrix} \cos m \theta _{1} & - \sin m \theta _{1} & & & \\ \sin m \theta _{1} & \cos m \theta _{1} & & & \\ & & \cos m \theta _{2} & -\sin m \theta _{2} & \\ & & \sin m \theta _{2} & \cos m \theta _{2} & \\ & & & & \ddots \end {bmatrix} \]

    Remark. The \(\theta _i\)'s are fixed and chosen according to some schedule to capture different "frequencies".

    The embedding are used by appling them to the query and key matrices separately i.e. \[W_K = \mathrm {ROT}(W_K), \quad W_Q = \mathrm {ROT}(W_Q)\] These are then what is used in self-attention.

      Definition. Field [X9AO]

      • July 14, 2025
      • Kellen Kanarios

      A field is a set \(F\) together with two binary operations on \(F\): \(+\) and \(\cdot \), where a binary operation is a mapping \(F \times F \to F\). These operations are defined by the following axioms

      • Associativity of addition and multiplication: \(a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c\), and \(a \cdot (b \cdot c) = (a \cdot b) \cdot c\).
      • Commutativity of addition and multiplication: \(a + b = b + a\), and \(a \cdot b = b \cdot a\).
      • Additive and multiplicative identity: there exist two distinct elements 0 and 1 in \(F\) such that \(a + 0 = a\) and \(a \cdot 1 = a\).
      • Additive inverses: for every \(a\) in \(F\), there exists an element in \(F\), denoted \(-a\), called the additive inverse of a, such that \(a + (-a) = 0\).
      • Multiplicative inverses: for every \(a \neq 0\) in \(F\), there exists an element in \(F\), denoted by \(a^{-1}\) or \(1/a\), called the multiplicative inverse of \(a\), such that \(a \cdot a^{-1} = 1\).
      • Distributivity of multiplication over addition: \(a \cdot (b + c) = (a \cdot b) + (a \cdot c)\).
