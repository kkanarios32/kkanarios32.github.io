  • January 26, 2025

The subdifferential is the set of all subgradient's of \(f\) at \(\boldsymbol {x}_0\). \[\partial f(\boldsymbol {x}_0) \coloneqq \{\boldsymbol {u} | f(\boldsymbol {x}) \geq f(\boldsymbol {x}_0) + \langle \boldsymbol {u}, \boldsymbol {x} - \boldsymbol {x}_0 \rangle \}\]

    Operations that preserve convexity [DER0]

    • July 6, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    The following operations preserve convexity

    • Nonnegative weighted sums. if \(f_1, \ldots , f_n\) are convex and \(\alpha _1, \ldots , \alpha _n > 0\) then we have \(f(\boldsymbol {x}) = \sum _{i = 1}^{n} \alpha _i f_i(\boldsymbol {x})\) is convex
    • Pointwise maximum. if \(f_1, \ldots , f_n\) are convex then \(f(\boldsymbol {x}) = \max _i \{f_i(\boldsymbol {x})\}\) is convex

    Basic Matrix Analysis [DER1]

    • July 6, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    A hub for the linear-algebra facts that recur throughout the optimization notes — inner products and traces, positive-definiteness, and the family of matrix norms built on the singular values.

    Definition. Matrix inner product [WWMR]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    The inner product on \(\mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\), obtained by treating a matrix as a long vector and summing the entrywise products: \[\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {Z}\rangle \ :=\ \sum _{i=1}^{m}\sum _{j=1}^{n}X_{ij}Z_{ij},\] the direct generalization of the vector case \(\langle \mathbf {x},\mathbf {z}\rangle =\sum _i x_i z_i\). It is the inner product whose induced norm is the Frobenius norm, \(\|\mathbf {X}\|_F=\sqrt {\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {X}\rangle }\).

    It has a compact trace form (see Matrix trace): \[\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {Z}\rangle \ =\ \mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}^{\top }\mathbf {Z})\ =\ \mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}\mathbf {Z}^{\top }).\]

    Definition. Matrix trace [PDX6]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    For a square matrix \(\mathbf {M}\in \mathbb {R}^{n\times n}\), the trace is the sum of its diagonal entries: \[\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {M}):=\sum _{i=1}^{n}M_{ii}.\]

    Two identities make it the workhorse of matrix calculus. It is invariant under cyclic permutation of a product, \[\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {A}\mathbf {B})=\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {B}\mathbf {A}),\qquad \mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {A}_{1}\mathbf {A}_{2}\cdots \mathbf {A}_{n})=\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {A}_{\pi (1)}\cdots \mathbf {A}_{\pi (n)})\] for any cyclic permutation \(\pi \) of \(\{1,\dots ,n\}\); and it computes the matrix inner product, \[\langle \mathbf {X},\mathbf {Z}\rangle =\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}^{\top }\mathbf {Z})=\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {X}\mathbf {Z}^{\top }).\]

    Definition. Positive (semi)definiteness [003A]

    • January 26, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    A symmetric matrix \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{n\times n}\) is positive definite if \(\mathbf {x}^{\top }\mathbf {A}\mathbf {x}>0\) for all nonzero \(\mathbf {x}\in \mathbb {R}^{n}\). It is positive semidefinite (p.s.d.) if \(\mathbf {x}^{\top }\mathbf {A}\mathbf {x}\geq 0\) for all \(\mathbf {x}\in \mathbb {R}^{n}\).

    Fact. Singular Value Decomposition [003B]

    • January 26, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\) with \(\operatorname {rank}(\mathbf {A})=r\). There exist \(\sigma _{1}\geq \sigma _{2}\geq \cdots \geq \sigma _{r}>0\), and matrices \[\mathbf {U}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times r},\ \mathbf {V}\in \mathbb {R}^{n\times r},\] with orthonormal columns \((\mathbf {U}^{\top }\mathbf {U}=\mathbf {I},\ \mathbf {V}^{\top }\mathbf {V}=\mathbf {I})\) such that \[\mathbf {A}\ =\ \mathbf {U}\mathbf {\Sigma }\mathbf {V}^{\top }\ =\ \sum _{i=1}^{r}\sigma _{i}\mathbf {u}_{i}\mathbf {v}_{i}^{\top }\,,\quad \mathbf {\Sigma }\ =\ \operatorname {diag}\left (\sigma _{1},\cdots ,\sigma _{r}\right ).\]

    Relationship between eigen decomposition and SVD

    • The columns of \(\boldsymbol {V}\) are eigenvectors of \(\boldsymbol {A}^T\boldsymbol {A}\)
    • The columns of \(\boldsymbol {U}\) are eigenvectors of \(\boldsymbol {A}\boldsymbol {A}^T\)
    • \({{\sigma _{i}({\mathbf {A}})}}={\sqrt {\lambda _{i}({{{\mathbf {A}}{\mathbf {A}}^{\mathsf {T}}}})}}={\sqrt {\lambda _{i}({\mathbf {A}}^{\mathsf {T}}{\mathbf {A}})}}\)

    Theorem. Best rank-\(r\) approximation [003C]

    • January 26, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\) with SVD, \(\mathbf {A}=\sum _{i=1}^{\min \{m,n\}}\sigma _{i}\mathbf {u}_{i}\mathbf {v}_{i}^{\top }\). Then an optimal solution to the best rank-\(r\) approximation problem \[\min _{\mathbf {X}}||\mathbf {X}-\mathbf {A}||_{2}\,,\quad \text {s.t.}\quad \text {rank}(\mathbf {X})\ \leq \ r\] is the truncated SVD of the following form \[\boxed {\widehat {\mathbf {A}}_{r}\ =\ \sum _{i=1}^{r}\sigma _{i}\mathbf {u}_{i}\mathbf {v}_{i}^{\top }}\,,\ \ \text {if}\ \ \sigma _{r}>\sigma _{r+1}\,,\,\text {it is unique.}\]

    Proof. Suppose \(m \geq n\), then we observe that \[\left |\left |{\widehat {\mathbf {A}}}_{r}-{\mathbf {A}}\right |\right |_{2}\;=\;\left |\left |\sum _{i=r+1}^{n}\sigma _{i}\mathbf {u}_{i}\mathbf {v}_{i}^{\mathsf {T}}\right |\right |_{2} \leq \sigma _{r+1}\] Second, to prove our result, we want to show that \[\left |\left |\mathbf {B}_{r}-\mathbf {A}\right |\right |_{2}\ \geq \ \sigma _{r+1},\quad \forall \mathbf {B}_{r}\ \text {with}\ \mathrm {rank}(\mathbf {B}_{r})=r.\] We suppose \(\ \mathbf {B}_{r}=\widetilde {\mathbf {U}}\widetilde {\mathbf {V}}^{\top },\quad \widetilde {\mathbf {U}}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times r},\ \widetilde {\mathbf {V}}\in \mathbb {R}^{n\times r}.\) Note \[ \begin {align*} ||\mathbf {B}_r - A||_2^2 &= \sup _{||\boldsymbol {z}||_2 \leq 1} ||(\mathbf {B}_r - \mathbf {A})\mathbf {z}||_2^2 \\ &\geq ||(\mathbf {B}_r - \mathbf {A})\mathbf {w}||_2^2 \end {align*} \] Since \(\widetilde {V}\) has \(r\) columns, then there must be a nontrivial linear combination of the first \(r{+}1\) columns of \(V\), i.e., \[\mathbf {w}=\gamma _{1}\mathbf {v}_{1}+\cdots +\gamma _{r+1}\mathbf {v}_{r+1}\] such that \(\widetilde {V}^{\top }\mathbf {w}=\mathbf {0}\,.\) Wlog, we can scale \(\left |\left |\mathbf {w}\right |\right |_{2}=1\) and \(\,\sum _{i=1}^{r+1}\gamma _{i}^{2}=1\) Then \[||\mathbf {A}-\mathbf {B}_{k}||_{2}^{2}\geq ||(\mathbf {A}-\mathbf {B}_{k})\,\mathbf {w}||_{2}^{2}=||\mathbf {A}\mathbf {w}||_{2}^{2}=\gamma _{1}^{2}\sigma _{1}^{2}+\cdots +\gamma _{r+1}^{2}\sigma _{r+1}^{2}\geq \sigma _{r+1}^{2}.\]

    Definition. Matrix Operator Norm [003G]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\). If \(\left |\left |\cdot \right |\right |_{a}\) and \(\left |\left |\cdot \right |\right |_{b}\) are norms on \(\mathbb {R}^{n}\) and \(\mathbb {R}^{m}\), we have \[\left |\left |\mathbf {A}\right |\right |_{a\to b}\ :=\ \sup _{\left |\left |\mathbf {x}\right |\right |_{a}\leq 1}\left |\left |\mathbf {A}\mathbf {x}\right |\right |_{b}.\] It satisfies the three criteria for norm, and is submultiplicative \[||\boldsymbol {A}\boldsymbol {B}||_{a\to b}\ \leq \ ||\boldsymbol {A}||_{a\to b}\cdot ||\boldsymbol {B}||_{a\to b};\]. \[||{\cal A}||_{2\to 2}\;=\;\sigma _{1}({\cal A})\;\;(\mathrm {{spectral\ norm}},\;\mathrm {{write\ as}}\;||{\cal A}||\;\;);\;\] \[\begin {array}{l}{{||{\boldsymbol {A}}||_{1\to b}\ =\ \operatorname *{max}_{j=1,\cdots ,n}||{\boldsymbol {A e}}_{j}||_{b}}}\\ {{||{\boldsymbol {A}}||_{a\to \infty }\ =\ \operatorname *{max}_{i=1,\cdots ,m}||{\boldsymbol {e}}_{i}^{*}{\boldsymbol {A}}||_{b}^{*}\,,\ \ ||{\boldsymbol {v}}||_{b}^{*}\ :=\ \operatorname *{sup}_{||{\boldsymbol {u}}||_{b}\leq 1}\langle {\boldsymbol {u}},{\boldsymbol {v}}\rangle \,,}}\end {array}\]

    Definition. Unitary Invariant Matrix Norm [003H]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times n}.\) We say the matrix norm is unitary invariant if \[\left ||\mathbf {A}\right ||_{\sharp }\ =\ \left ||\mathbf {PAQ}\right ||_{\sharp },\quad \forall \ \mathbf {P}\in O(m),\ \mathbf {Q}\in O(n).\]

    The two most common examples are both unitary invariant because they depend on \(\mathbf {A}\) only through its singular values: the Spectral norm (\(\|\sigma (\mathbf {A})\|_\infty \)) and the Frobenius norm (\(\|\sigma (\mathbf {A})\|_2\)).

    Definition. Spectral norm [3U43]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    The operator norm of a matrix induced by the Euclidean norm on both sides, \(\|\mathbf {A}\|_{2\to 2}\), usually written just \(\|\mathbf {A}\|\). It equals the largest singular value, \[\|\mathbf {A}\|\ =\ \sup _{\|\mathbf {x}\|_2\leq 1}\|\mathbf {A}\mathbf {x}\|_2\ =\ \sigma _1(\mathbf {A}),\] i.e. the most any unit vector can be stretched by \(\mathbf {A}\). As a Schatten norm it is the \(p=\infty \) case, \(\|\mathbf {A}\|_{\mathrm {S}_\infty }=\|\sigma (\mathbf {A})\|_\infty \), sitting opposite the Frobenius norm (\(p=2\)) in that family.

    Definition. Frobenius norm [4M9J]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    The entrywise Euclidean norm of a matrix — treat \(\mathbf {A}\) as a long vector and take its 2-norm: \[\|\mathbf {A}\|_F\ =\ \sqrt {\sum _{i,j}A_{ij}^2}\ =\ \sqrt {\langle \mathbf {A},\mathbf {A}\rangle }\ =\ \sqrt {\mathrm {tr}(\mathbf {A}^{\top }\mathbf {A})}.\] It is the norm induced by the matrix inner product, and in terms of singular values it is the \(p=2\) Schatten norm, \(\|\mathbf {A}\|_F=\sqrt {\sum _i\sigma _i^2(\mathbf {A})}\) — the Euclidean length of the singular-value vector, dual in that family to the spectral norm (\(p=\infty \)).

    Definition. Schatten \(p\)-norm [003I]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(\mathbf {A}\in \mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\). For any \(p \in [1,+\infty ]\), the function \[\left ||\mathbf {A}|\right |_{\mathrm {S}_{p}}\,:=\,\left ||\mathbf {\sigma }(\mathbf {A})|\right |_{p}\] is a norm on \(\,\mathbb {R}^{m\times n}\).

    Definition. Lipschitz Continous [003J]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Suppose that

    • \(f:\mathcal {X}\mapsto \mathcal {Y}\) with \(\mathcal {X}\) and \(\mathcal {Y}\) being open sets;
    • \(||\cdot ||_{\mathcal {X}}\) and \(||\cdot ||_{\mathcal {Y}}\) are norms on \(\mathcal {X}\) and \(\mathcal {Y}\), respectively.
    \(f(\cdot )\) is Lipschitz continuous over \(\mathcal {X}\) if \(\exists L<\infty \,,\) such that \[||f(\boldsymbol {y})-f(\boldsymbol {x})||_{\mathcal {Y}}\ \leq \ L(\boldsymbol {x})\,||\boldsymbol {y}-\boldsymbol {x}||_{\mathcal {X}}\,,\quad \forall \boldsymbol {x},\ \boldsymbol {y}\in \mathcal {X}.\]

    Definition. Smooth Function [003K]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    A differentiable function \(f(\mathbf {x})\) is called smooth iff it has a Lipschitz continuous gradient, i.e., iff \(L< \infty \) such that \[||\nabla f(\mathbf {x})-\nabla f(\mathbf {z}) ||_{2}\ \leq L ||\mathbf {x}-\mathbf {z} ||_{2},\quad \forall \mathbf {x},\ \mathbf {z}\in \mathbb {R}^{n}\]

    Theorem. Mean Value Theorem I [003L]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(f:\mathbb {R}^{n}\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) be continuously differentiable. For any fixed \(\mathbf {x}\) and \(\mathbf {y}\), we have \[\,f(\mathbf {y})\,=\,f(\mathbf {x})\,+\,\langle \nabla f(\mathbf {z}(t_{L})),\mathbf {y}-\mathbf {x}\rangle \,\] for some \(\mathbf {z}(t_{L})=(1-t_{L})\cdot \mathbf {x}+t_{L}\cdot \mathbf {y}\) with \(t_{L}\in (0,1)\,\).

    Remark.
    • If \(f:\mathbb {R}^n\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) is continuously differentiable, \[\begin {aligned} |f(\pmb {x})-f(\pmb {y})| &\leq & \sup _{\pmb {z} \in \mathbb {R}^n} ||\nabla f(\pmb {z})||_2 \, ||\pmb {x}-\pmb {y}||_2 \end {aligned}\] so that the Lipschitz constant of \(f\) is \(\sup _{\pmb {z} \in \mathbb {R}^n} ||\nabla f(\pmb {z})||_2\).
    • If \(f:\mathbb {R}^{n}\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) is twice continuously differentiable, \[||\nabla f(\boldsymbol {x})-\nabla f(\boldsymbol {y})||_{2}\ \leq \ \sup _{\boldsymbol {z}\in \mathbb {R}^{n}}||\nabla ^{2}f(\boldsymbol {z})||_{2}\,||\boldsymbol {x}-\boldsymbol {y}||_{2}\] so that the Lipschitz constant of \(\nabla f\) is \(\sup _{\boldsymbol {z}\in \mathbb {R}^{n}}||\nabla ^{2}f(\boldsymbol {z})||_{2}\)

    Theorem. Mean Value Theorem II [003M]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(f:\mathbb {R}^{n}\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) be twice continuously differentiable. For any fixed \(\mathbf {x}\) and \(\mathbf {y}\), we have \[\nabla f(\mathbf {y})\;=\;\nabla f(\mathbf {x})\,+\,\int _{0}^{1}\nabla ^{2}f(\mathbf {z}(t))\cdot (\mathbf {y}-\mathbf {x})dt\] for some \(\mathbf {z}(t)=(1-t)\mathbf {x}+t\mathbf {y}\) with \(t\in (0,1)\,,\) and that \[\boxed {f(\mathbf {y})\;=\;f(\mathbf {x})+(\nabla f(\mathbf {x}),\mathbf {y}-\mathbf {x})+\frac {1}{2}(\mathbf {y}-\mathbf {x})^{\top }\nabla ^{2}f(\mathbf {z}(t_{Q}))(\mathbf {y}-\mathbf {x})}\] for some \(t_{Q}\in (0,1)\,.\)

    Optimality Conditions [DER3]

    • July 6, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Local conditions that a minimizer of a (not necessarily convex) \(f\) must satisfy, phrased in terms of its gradient and Hessian at the candidate point. They pin an optimum down to a stationary point and then use curvature to separate minima from maxima and saddles.

    Definition. Stationary point [003P]

    • January 27, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    A point \(\mathbf {x_{\star }}\) is a stationary point of \(f(\cdot )\), if \[\nabla f(\mathbf {x_{\star }})\ =\ \mathbf {0}.\] Here, \(\mathbf {x_{\star }}\) is also known as a critical point.

    Theorem. First-order optimality condition [I6DT]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    If \(\mathbf {x_\star }\) is a local minimizer of \(f:\mathbb {R}^n\to \mathbb {R}\) and \(f\) is continuously differentiable in an open neighborhood of \(\mathbf {x_\star }\), then \(\mathbf {x_\star }\) is a stationary point: \[\nabla f(\mathbf {x_\star })\ =\ \mathbf {0}.\]

    This is only necessary: a stationary point may be a maximizer or saddle, which is what the second-order conditions disambiguate. Under convexity it becomes sufficient as well — a stationary point of a convex function is a global minimizer.

    Theorem. Second-order optimality conditions [U3L4]

    • July 11, 2026
    • Kellen Kanarios

    Let \(f:\mathbb {R}^n\to \mathbb {R}\) be twice continuously differentiable in an open neighborhood of \(\mathbf {x_\star }\).

    Necessary. If \(\mathbf {x_\star }\) is a local minimizer, then \[(i)\ \nabla f(\mathbf {x_\star })=\mathbf {0};\qquad (ii)\ \nabla ^2 f(\mathbf {x_\star })\succeq \mathbf {0}.\] That is, it is a stationary point whose Hessian is positive semidefinite.

    Sufficient. If instead \(\nabla f(\mathbf {x_\star })=\mathbf {0}\) and \(\nabla ^2 f(\mathbf {x_\star })\succ \mathbf {0}\) (Hessian strictly positive definite), then \(\mathbf {x_\star }\) is a strict local minimizer. The gap between \(\succeq \) and \(\succ \) is exactly the ambiguity the first-order condition alone cannot resolve.

    Remark. Let \(f:\mathbb {R}^{n}\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) be a convex function, then
    • A local minimizer of \(f\) is also its global minimizer. If \(f\) is strictly convex, the global minimizer is unique
    • A point is a global minimizer of \(f\) iff \[\mathbf {0}\ \in \ \partial f(\mathbf {x_{\star }}).\]
    If \(f\in \mathcal {C}^{1}\), then \(\nabla f(\mathbf {x_{\star }})=\mathbf {0}\) implies that \(\mathbf {x_{\star }}\) is a global minimizer.

    Lemma. Sufficient Value [004L]

    • February 3, 2025
    • Kellen Kanarios

    With step size \(\tau _{k}=1/L\,,\) the gradient descent iterates \(\{\mathbf {x}_{k}\}_{k\geq 1}\) generated by \[\mathbf {x}_{k+1}\ =\ \mathbf {x}_{k}\ -\ \tau _{k}\cdot \nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\] satisfy the following: \[f(\mathbf {x}_{k+1})\ \leq \ f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\ -\ \frac {1}{2L}\,||\nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k})||_{2}^{2}\] \[<\ f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\quad (||\nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k})||_{2}\neq 0)\]

    Proof. Because \(\nabla f\) is Lipschitz, by 1st-order Taylor approximation \[f(\mathbf {x}^{\prime })\leq f(\mathbf {x})+\langle \nabla f(\mathbf {x}),\mathbf {x}^{\prime }-\mathbf {x}\rangle +\frac {L}{2}\left ||\mathbf {x}^{\prime }-\mathbf {x}\right ||_{2}^{2},\quad \forall \ \mathbf {x}^{\prime },\mathbf {x}\] Now take \(\mathbf {x}' = \mathbf {x} - \tau \nabla f( \mathbf {x})\). Then \[ \begin {align*} f(\mathbf {x}^{\prime }) &\leq f(\mathbf {x})+ \tau ||\nabla f( \mathbf {x} )||_2^2 + \frac {\tau ^2 L}{2} ||\nabla f( \mathbf {x})||_2^2 \\ &= f( \mathbf {x}) - \tau (1 - \frac {\tau L}{2})||\nabla f( \mathbf {x})||_2^2 \end {align*} \] Take \(\tau = \frac {1}{L}\), such that \[ \begin {align*} f( \mathbf {x}) - \tau (1 - \frac {\tau L}{2})||\nabla f( \mathbf {x})||_2^2 &= f( \mathbf {x}) - \frac {1}{2L}||\nabla f( \mathbf {x})||_2^2 \end {align*} \]

    Theorem. Sublinear convergence of GD [004M]

    • February 3, 2025

    Suppose \(f:\mathbb {R}^{n}\mapsto \mathbb {R}\) is convex and smooth with its gradient \(L\)-Lipschitz.
