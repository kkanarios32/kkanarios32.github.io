Newton's Method [004V]

  • February 3, 2025

Consider second-order taylor approximation of \(f\) \[ f(\mathbf {x}_{k}+\mathbf {d})\ \approx \ \underbrace {f(\mathbf {x}_{k})+\mathbf {d}^{\top }\nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k})+{\frac {1}{2}}\mathbf {d}^{\top }\nabla ^{2}f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\mathbf {d}}_{Q_k( \mathbf {d})}\] Then setting \(\nabla Q_k( \mathbf {d})\) and solving for \(\mathbf {d}\), we get \[ \nabla f( \mathbf {x}_k) + \frac {1}{2}\nabla ^2 f( \mathbf {x}_k) \mathbf {d} = 0 \] Thus, take Newton's direction \[\mathbf {d}_{k}^{N}=-\left (\nabla ^{2}f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\right )^{-1}\nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k}).\]

Remark. Some notes
  • Fast convergence. Newton's method usually has local quadratic convergence.
  • Expensive. requires storing and inverting \(\nabla ^2 f( \mathbf {x})\).
  • Unstable. \(\nabla ^2 f \succ 0\) might not hold for general nonlinear problems.

    Quasi-Newton's Method [004W]

    • February 3, 2025

    Get around computing and inverting hessian by using a surrogate i.e. \[\mathbf {d}_{k}^{QN}=-\underbrace {\mathbf {H}_k}_{\text {surrogate for } \left (\nabla ^{2}f(\mathbf {x}_{k})\right )^{-1}} \nabla f(\mathbf {x}_{k}).\] Want \[{\mathbf {H}}_{k+1}^{-1}\cdot \underbrace {({\mathbf {x}}_{k+1}-{\mathbf {x}}_{k})}_{{\mathbf {s}}_{k}}\;=\;\underbrace {\nabla f({\mathbf {x}}_{k+1})-\nabla f({\mathbf {x}}_{k})}_{{\mathbf {y}}_{k}}\] BFGS rank-2 update: \[{\mathbf {H}}_{k+1}\ =\ {\mathbf {H}}_{k}+{\frac {\left ({\mathbf {s}}_{k}^{\top }{\mathbf {y}}_{k}+{\mathbf {y}}_{k}^{\top }{\mathbf {H}}_{k}{\mathbf {y}}_{k}\right ){\mathbf {s}}_{k}{\mathbf {s}}_{k}^{\top }}{\left ({\mathbf {s}}_{k}^{\top }{\mathbf {y}}_{k}\right )^{2}}}-{\frac {{\mathbf {H}}_{k}{\mathbf {y}}_{k}{\mathbf {s}}_{k}^{\top }+{\mathbf {s}}_{k}{\mathbf {y}}_{k}^{\top }{\mathbf {H}}_{k}}{{\mathbf {s}}_{k}^{\top }{\mathbf {y}}_{k}}}\]
